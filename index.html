Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

  • Cyclic Nature of New Cases: Thi graphic shows that the new cases tend to spike every 5-8 months. This is interesting since most infectious diseases tend to have a seasonal pattern, but this data shows a more cyclic pattern
  • Relatively Small Peaks leading Big Peaks: The graphic shows that there is a consistent pattern of a smaller peak before a larger peak. This is seen with the small July 2020 peak before the large January 2021 peak, and the September 2021 peak before the even larger January 2022 peak.
  • Peaks The highest peak seems to be at the edge of the datasets limits in January 2022, at an estimate 300K cases
  • Troughs: In terms of lowest new cases, June of the 2021 had the least amount of new cases. However, this led to large spike in September of 2021

Design Critique:

The visualization demonstrates several strengths, particularly in its use of a year-long cycle format which makes month-to-month and seasonal comparisons across years intuitive and accessible. Another strength is it's color, which employs a soft red that is easy on the eyes and effectively differentiates the black center mean lines. Although color could be used more strategically to enhance the message. Additionally, the labeling is fairly clear and comprehensive, providing a well-communicated title and legend to guide interpretation. However, the visualization has areas for improvement. Quantifying exact values is challenging, as the design focuses more on general trends than precise numbers. The legend's range of values, which states a maximum of 150K cases, creates confusion, especially when peaks in January 2021 and January 2022 exceed this range, leaving it unclear whether the legend reflects the plot's maximum size or an upper limit surpassed by the data. The placement of the "7-day average" label within the data section also reduces its visibility and may cause confusion, as it seems misplaced and better suited within the title. Regarding the month labels, it may be difficult for a viewer to interpolate months when only given 4 througout the year. This is dificult since the viewer would have think about more since months increase from right to left on the bottom half as opposed to right to left. Furthermore, the spiraled data format adds complexity, making it difficult for the brain to follow patterns within a single year without inadvertently jumping to another year's data due to the overlapping circular and radial directions.

Final Visualization Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

The goal of this plot is to provide a straightforward way to highlight the greatest peaks of new COVID-19 cases and to show that there was often a smaller relative peak preceding each larger one. I found this fact interesting, and was the reason I wanted to continue working with the new US Cases data instead of any of the other data in the dataset. I also found it interesting that there was a negative number of new cases on June 4th, 2021. This was not aparent in the original post, but was a clear outlier in the data when visualize in the new approach. I wanted to make sure that this was still visible in the final plot. (I was unable to find an explanation for this negative number, but this would be a point of continued investigation). Importantly, this design subtly reminds the viewer of the spike protein after which COVID-19 is named. The prominent "spikes" stand out as alarming elements, color-coded in red to indicate times of concern. I decided to keep the color associated with the lowest amounts of new cases a yellow instead of a green as in the handwritten drafts to help signify that any new cases can be a danger to life. I experimented with a logarithmic scale to better display the smaller spikes since these are seen as minimal in this plot. However, to emphasize the peaks in the data, I decided to retain a linear scale. It's worth noting that the periodic nature and other patterns may be obscured due to the circular shape, which can be challenging for the eye to follow.Nonetheless, despite its simplicity, I believe the plot effectively meets the goal of illustrating periods of concern regarding new cases.

Reflection

I really enjoyed creating my final product, as I believe it strikes a good balance between simplifying the plot's design and effectively conveying the desired message. The biggest challenge I wish could be further explored is how to easily compare the seasonality of the data; this was a clear advantage of the concentric circle and spiral plots but was lost when the years were consolidated into a single circle. However, transitioning to a singular circle made the visualization significantly easier to read which addressed one of original critiques of the plot. I also think being able to include the title, legend, and "7 day avg" information within the plot ensures that the viewer can quickly find all necessary context for the graph quickly, which was also a previous critique. I think that combining the use of color with the plot's magnitude helped communicate to a wider audience, accommodating different viewer preferences and considering potential colorblindness. This really helped the viewer find peaks even quicker than the original plot, really demonstrating the desired message without loosing clarity or simplicity. Overall, although the original plot was looking to address a different question, this final visualization was able to address many of the critiques that I had previous brought up.
